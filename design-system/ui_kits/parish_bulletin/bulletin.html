Eleventh Sunday in Ordinary Time
June 14, 2026 · Ordinary Time

“Her many sins have been forgiven; hence, she has shown great love.”

Luke 7:47

FH

From Fr. Francisco

Friends, this Sunday’s Gospel finds Jesus at dinner in the home of a Pharisee, where an uninvited woman weeps at his feet. The room is quick to judge her. Jesus is not. He sees her love, and he names it out loud.

It is easy, in a small town, to know each other’s histories — who stumbled, who drifted, who came back. The Lord asks us to see one another the way he saw that woman: not by the worst day, but by the love that brings us through the door of the church at all.

This week, when you pass the peace at Mass, look the person in the eye. You may be looking at someone the rest of the world wrote off, whom God has forgiven and set free.

Who is the one person this week I find hardest to forgive — and what would it cost me to begin?

Saint of the Week

St. Anthony of Padua

Saint image

A Franciscan friar and gifted preacher, Anthony (1195–1231) was so steeped in Scripture that he could quote it from memory at length. He is the patron of lost things — though he himself sought only to find souls for Christ.

His feast falls on June 13. Ask his prayers this week for whatever, or whomever, feels lost to you.

Feast · June 13 · Patron of the lost

For little ones St. Anthony loved to read God’s word. Ask a grown-up to help you find one Bible verse this week and learn it by heart.

Why We Do This

Ordinary Time & the Color Green

The green vestments of Ordinary Time are not a pause between the “important” seasons. Green is the color of growth — of the long, fruitful stretch when the Church walks day by day with the Lord through his public ministry.

These are the weeks for steady, ordinary faithfulness: daily prayer, weekly Mass, small mercies. Holiness is mostly built here, in the green.

Catechism Corner

On God’s Mercy

“There is no one, however wicked and guilty, who may not confidently hope for forgiveness, provided his repentance is honest.”

CCC 1864

In plain words: no sin is bigger than God’s mercy. The only thing mercy asks of us is an honest heart that turns back.

✠

Lord Jesus, you saw the woman’s love where others saw only her sin. Give me your eyes, that I may see mercy before I see fault, and love before I keep count. Amen.
